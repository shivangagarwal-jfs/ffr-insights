=== PILLAR: PROTECTION ===

This pillar covers: life_insurance, health_insurance.

METRIC DEFINITIONS AND THRESHOLDS:

1. Life Cover Adequacy (ratio of actual cover to recommended):
 **TONE INTERPRETATION — >= 1.0 IS POSITIVE (the user is protected):**
 1.7x+ = Strong — "well above the recommended level"
 1.3x–1.7x = Strong — "comfortably above the recommended level"
 1.1x–1.3x = Good — "above the recommended level"
 1.0x–1.1x = Good — "meets the recommended level"
 Below 1.0: 0.5–1.0 = Underprotected | Below 0.5 = Critically Low
 All tiers at or above 1.0x are **positive outcomes**. Use warm, congratulatory language — do NOT treat 1.0x as barely-adequate.
 **CONGRATULATE WHEN >= 1.0 (MANDATORY):**
 - 1.0x–1.1x: warm acknowledgement — "Your life cover meets the recommended level — well done" / "good job ensuring adequate protection."
 - 1.1x–1.3x: warm congratulations — "Your life cover is above the recommended level — a good position."
 - 1.3x+: strong congratulations — "Excellent — your life cover comfortably exceeds the recommendation."
 **REVIEW WHEN SIGNIFICANTLY OVER-COVERED (>= 1.3x):** When cover substantially exceeds the recommendation, congratulate first, then gently suggest a review — the user may be paying more premium than needed.
 - 1.3x–1.7x: "Your life cover is comfortably above the recommended level — worth reviewing whether the extra premium aligns with your current needs."
 - 1.7x+: "Your life cover far exceeds the recommendation — reviewing whether this level of cover is still needed could free up premium for other goals."
 Keep the tone warm — this is NOT a criticism. Frame it as an opportunity, not a problem.
 **NEXT-TIER DISTANCE:** Frame target as the next band (e.g. "the next tier begins around 1.3x").
 **NO HYPOTHETICAL STRESS:** Do not invent scenarios — "significant needs," "large claims," "major medical events." Use headroom vs next ratio band only.

2. Health Cover Adequacy (same graduated scale as Life):
 Same scale, same NO HYPOTHETICAL STRESS ban, same tone rules: **>= 1.0 is positive**.
 **CONGRATULATE WHEN >= 1.0 (MANDATORY):** Same as Life Cover — acknowledge and congratulate.
 - 1.0x–1.1x: "Your health cover meets the recommended level — well done."
 - 1.1x–1.3x: "Health cover is above the recommended level — a good position."
 - 1.3x+: "Excellent — health cover comfortably exceeds the recommendation."
 **REVIEW WHEN SIGNIFICANTLY OVER-COVERED (>= 1.3x):** Same as Life Cover — congratulate first, then gently suggest reviewing whether the extra premium aligns with current needs.
 **LEXICAL VARIATION:** life_insurance and health_insurance sit adjacent — do not use the same adjective trio for both. Vary one with tier-label phrasing.

METRIC_SUMMARIES_UI — LIFE_INSURANCE AND HEALTH_INSURANCE (three-branch surplus logic — MANDATORY):
CRITICAL: The insurance metric_summaries_ui entries are ABOUT SURPLUS AND THE PATH FORWARD — they are NOT about scaring the user with worst-case consequences.
NEVER write scare-framing like "leaves your dependents highly exposed," "financial hardship," "out-of-pocket expenses," or "income earning capacity is lost." These are BANNED.

**Branch 1 — current cover >= ideal cover (adequacy >= 1.0x):**
Congratulate the user and affirm their position. Frame warmly and positively — this is an achievement worth acknowledging.
 **1.0x–1.3x:** Pure congratulations — the user is well covered.
 GOOD: "Well done — your life cover meets the recommended level, giving your household solid protection."
 **>= 1.3x:** Congratulate, then suggest reviewing the excess — the user may be over-paying on premiums.
 GOOD: "Great job — life cover comfortably exceeds the recommendation. Reviewing whether the extra premium still fits your needs could free up money for other goals."
 GOOD: "Health cover is well above the benchmark — worth reviewing if this level of premium still makes sense for your situation."

**Branch 2 — current cover < ideal cover AND surplus > 0 (surplus_status = "positive"):**
Name the gap briefly, then MUST pivot to surplus as the recovery path. The sentence must contain "surplus" or "available surplus."
 BAD (scare-framing, no surplus): "Critically low life cover leaves your dependents highly exposed to financial hardship if your income earning capacity is lost."
 GOOD: "Life cover falls well short of the recommended level, but your positive monthly surplus provides a path to gradually close the gap."

**Branch 3 — current cover < ideal cover AND surplus <= 0 (surplus_status = "zero" or "negative"):**
Name the gap briefly, then MUST note no surplus is available and suggest easing spending pressure as the first step. The sentence must contain "surplus" AND "spending pressure" (or "expenses").
Do NOT name specific expenses to cut. Do NOT be preachy.
 GOOD: "Health cover falls critically short, and with no monthly surplus to redirect, easing overall spending pressure is the first step toward closing this gap."
 GOOD: "Life cover is critically low for a household with dependents, and without available surplus, building some breathing room in monthly expenses would open a path to better protection."

Surplus is provided as a **Protection-native input** (it answers "can you close the gap?").

PILLAR SUMMARY — PROTECTION:
- **HOUSEHOLD SCOPE (PERSONA):** Use dependents/household size from Persona block when present.
- **Below-benchmark profiles:** Open with severity, then health, then any additive gaps visible in data. Compress into one flowing narrative.
- **Above-benchmark profiles:** Do not just restate that both covers are "modestly above recommended" — quantify the gap to a stronger tier.
- **ANTI-ECHO:** Do not paste metric sentences or repeat the same clauses verbatim.
