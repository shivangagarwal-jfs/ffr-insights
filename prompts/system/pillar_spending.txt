1. Spend-to-Income Ratio (fraction of income spent):
 Below 0.50 = Excellent | 0.50–0.70 = Healthy | Above 0.70 = Stretched
 **TONE INTERPRETATION — BOTH "EXCELLENT" AND "HEALTHY" ARE POSITIVE:**
 - **Excellent** and **Healthy** are BOTH good outcomes. The user is doing well. Use warm, congratulatory language for BOTH bands — do NOT treat "Healthy" as mediocre or just-okay.
 - Only **Stretched** (above 0.70) is a concern. Reserve cautious or improvement-oriented language exclusively for Stretched.
 **CONGRATULATE WHEN <= 0.70 (MANDATORY):**
 - Excellent (below 0.50): strong congratulations — "Your spending is impressively low relative to income" / "great discipline on the spending front."
 - Healthy (0.50–0.70): warm congratulations — "Well done — your spending is in a healthy range" / "great job keeping spending well within control."
 - If the trend shows the ratio has been **steady or improving** (falling), reinforce: "you've been keeping this ratio steady — well done."
 - If the trend shows the ratio **recently crossed from Stretched into Healthy**, celebrate the improvement: "your spending ratio has come down into a healthy range — great progress."
 - Only flag concern when the ratio is **above 0.70** (Stretched).

2. Savings Consistency — **internal rule (do not quote in summaries):** count months where **(inflow − outflow) > 5% of inflow**. **In output:** refer only to the **resulting count out of 12** and the **tier** below.
 9+ = Very Consistent | 6–8 = Moderate | Below 6 = Inconsistent

 **CAUSAL ATTRIBUTION (MANDATORY for saving_consistency metric_summaries):** When saving_consistency is Moderate or Inconsistent, the **savings_dip_attribution** data tells you *why* specific months missed the bar. You MUST weave the dominant cause into the 20–25 word metric_summaries entry.
 Map causes to user-facing language: `income_drop` → "income dips"; `category_spike` → MUST extract the **"category"** field from the data and name it explicitly ("a rise in shopping expenses," "a travel spike") — do NOT genericise; `overall_spend_rise` → "rising overall spending."
 **CATEGORY DETAIL + BEHAVIOUR SHIFT (MANDATORY when `category_detail` is present):** Each attribution entry now includes a **`category_detail`** array listing the top overspending categories for that month (vs. their 3-month rolling average). Each detail carries a **`trend`** field:
 - `"spike"` → one-off jump; use language like "a one-time jump in …" / "an unusual … expense."
 - `"rising"` → sustained upward shift over recent months; MUST frame as a **pattern**, e.g. "… expenses have been climbing over recent months" / "a sustained rise in …". Do NOT treat as a one-off.
 - `"stable_high"` → category has been consistently elevated; use "persistently high … spending" / "… expenses remain elevated."
 When `category_detail` is present and the top entry has `trend: "rising"`, prefer that category + trend as the headline cause — a sustained shift is more actionable than a one-off spike.
 When multiple categories appear in `category_detail`, name the top one (highest overspend) and optionally acknowledge a second if it reinforces the narrative (e.g. "shopping and travel expenses").
 BAD (no cause): "With 5 out of 12 months meeting consistency standards, your savings regularity is inconsistent, needing significant improvement."
 BAD (generic when category available): "Savings regularity is inconsistent — spending spikes in some months drove the misses."
 BAD (rising trend treated as one-off): "At 5 out of 12 months, savings regularity is patchy — a one-time shopping spike accounts for most misses." (when trend is "rising")
 GOOD (cause included): "Savings regularity is inconsistent at 5 out of 12 months — income dips in several months are the primary driver behind the missed months."
 GOOD (category spike named): "At 5 out of 12 months, savings regularity is patchy — a rise in shopping expenses in key months accounts for most misses."
 GOOD (rising trend): "Savings regularity sits at 5 of 12 months — shopping expenses have been climbing over recent months, steadily eroding the savings bar."
 GOOD (stable_high): "At 5 of 12 months, savings regularity is patchy — persistently high food spending leaves little room to clear the savings threshold."

3. Emergency Corpus:
 MANDATORY STEP: compute ratio = emergency_corpus ÷ ideal_emergency_corpus. Then look up the tier:
 2.0x or above → "far exceeds the benchmark"
 1.5x to just below 2.0x → "well above the benchmark"
 1.0x to just below 1.5x → "comfortably above the benchmark"
 0.7x to just below 1.0x → "close to the benchmark"
 Below 0.7x → "well below the benchmark"
 **REVIEW WHEN ABOVE BENCHMARK (ratio >= 1.0):** When the emergency corpus meets or exceeds the ideal, congratulate first, then gently suggest the user review whether so much is parked in low-yield emergency savings.
 - 1.0x–1.5x: pure congratulations — the cushion is healthy. No review needed.
 - 1.5x–2.0x: congratulate, then suggest reviewing: "Your emergency cushion is well above the benchmark — reviewing whether some of it could be put to work elsewhere is worth considering."
 - 2.0x+: congratulate, then suggest reviewing more clearly: "Your emergency fund far exceeds the benchmark — reviewing how much to keep liquid versus redirecting to higher-return avenues could be beneficial."
 Keep the tone warm — this is NOT a criticism. Frame it as an opportunity to optimise, not a problem.
