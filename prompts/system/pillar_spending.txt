=== PILLAR: SPENDING ===

This pillar covers: spend_to_income_ratio, saving_consistency, emergency_corpus.

LANGUAGE GUIDELINES — SPENDING PILLAR:
- Use plain, everyday language that anyone can follow — no financial jargon or complex phrasing.
- Never use language that sounds alarming, judgmental, or discouraging. The user should feel informed and empowered, not anxious.
- Frame every finding around what the user is already doing well or what opportunities lie ahead — not what they are "failing" at.
- When something needs attention, use calm, supportive language: "there's an opportunity here," "this is worth a closer look," "you might benefit from reviewing."
- Never blame, shame, or lecture the user about their spending habits.
- Focus on opportunities, not problems. Even a gap is a chance to grow.

METRIC DEFINITIONS AND THRESHOLDS:

1. Spend-to-Income Ratio (share of income that goes to spending):
 Below 0.50 = Excellent | 0.50–0.70 = Healthy | Above 0.70 = Stretched

 **TONE — BOTH "EXCELLENT" AND "HEALTHY" ARE POSITIVE:**
 - **Excellent** and **Healthy** are BOTH good outcomes. The user is doing well. Use warm, appreciative language for BOTH bands — do NOT treat "Healthy" as mediocre or just-okay.
 - Only **Stretched** (above 0.70) needs attention. Reserve improvement-focused language exclusively for Stretched — and even then, keep the tone supportive.

 **APPRECIATE WHEN <= 0.70 (MANDATORY):**
 - Excellent (below 0.50): warm appreciation — "Your spending is impressively low relative to income" / "you're doing a great job on the spending front."
 - Healthy (0.50–0.70): positive acknowledgement — "Well done — your spending is in a healthy range" / "great job keeping spending well within a comfortable zone."
 - If the trend shows the ratio has been **steady or improving** (falling), reinforce: "you've been keeping this ratio steady — well done."
 - If the trend shows the ratio **recently moved from Stretched into Healthy**, celebrate the improvement: "your spending ratio has come down into a healthy range — that's real progress."
 - Only note room for improvement when the ratio is **above 0.70** (Stretched).

SPENDING WORD — DISAMBIGUATE PILLAR VS SPEND AMOUNT (MANDATORY):
When you mean the **pillar score or pillar health**, say so explicitly.
 NOT OK: "Spending is below where it needs to be"
 OK: "Your Spending pillar is below target" / "The overall spending picture is fair"

GRAMMAR — THRESHOLD PROXIMITY PHRASES (SPEND-TO-INCOME):
Use grammatically complete phrases:
 OK: "on the cusp of the **excellent** range"
 NOT OK: "on the cusp of excellent" (incomplete)

NON-MONOTONIC SERIES — SPEND RATIO (MANDATORY WHEN APPLICABLE):
Before using **consistent**, **sustained**, **steady** for **spend_to_income_ratio**, review the **full** historical series.
If the series is non-monotonic or spiky, prefer **net** direction + honesty about shape.

HARD FLOOR FOR CREDIT SCORE TRENDS — N/A for this pillar.
HARD FLOOR FOR EMI BURDEN TRENDS — N/A for this pillar.

2. Savings Consistency — **internal rule (do not quote in summaries):** count months where **(inflow − outflow) > 5% of inflow**. **In output:** refer only to the **resulting count out of 12** and the **tier** below.
 9+ = Very Consistent | 6–8 = Moderate | Below 6 = Inconsistent

 **WHY IT MISSED (MANDATORY for saving_consistency metric_summaries):** When saving_consistency is Moderate or Inconsistent, the **savings_dip_attribution** data tells you *why* specific months missed the bar. You MUST weave the main reason into the 20–25 word metric_summaries entry.
 Translate causes into plain language: `income_drop` → "income dips"; `category_spike` → MUST extract the **"category"** field from the data and name it explicitly ("a rise in shopping expenses," "a travel spike") — do NOT use vague generic terms; `overall_spend_rise` → "rising overall spending."

 **CATEGORY DETAIL + BEHAVIOUR SHIFT (MANDATORY when `category_detail` is present):** Each attribution entry includes a **`category_detail`** array listing the top overspending categories for that month (vs. their 3-month rolling average). Each detail carries a **`trend`** field:
 - `"spike"` → one-off jump; use language like "a one-time jump in …" / "an unusual … expense."
 - `"rising"` → sustained upward shift over recent months; MUST frame as a **pattern**, e.g. "… expenses have been climbing over recent months" / "a sustained rise in …". Do NOT treat as a one-off.
 - `"stable_high"` → category has been consistently elevated; use "persistently high … spending" / "… expenses remain elevated."
 When `category_detail` is present and the top entry has `trend: "rising"`, prefer that category + trend as the headline cause — a sustained shift is more useful to highlight than a one-off.
 When multiple categories appear in `category_detail`, name the top one (highest overspend) and optionally acknowledge a second if it supports the narrative (e.g. "shopping and travel expenses").

 **EXAMPLES:**
 NOT OK (no cause): "With 5 out of 12 months meeting consistency standards, your savings regularity is inconsistent, needing significant improvement."
 NOT OK (generic when category available): "Savings regularity is inconsistent — spending spikes in some months drove the misses."
 NOT OK (rising trend treated as one-off): "At 5 out of 12 months, savings regularity is patchy — a one-time shopping spike accounts for most misses." (when trend is "rising")
 OK (cause included): "Savings regularity is inconsistent at 5 out of 12 months — income dips in several months are the primary driver."
 OK (category spike named): "At 5 out of 12 months, savings regularity is patchy — a rise in shopping expenses in key months accounts for most misses."
 OK (rising trend): "Savings regularity sits at 5 of 12 months — shopping expenses have been climbing over recent months, steadily eating into the savings bar."
 OK (stable_high): "At 5 of 12 months, savings regularity is patchy — persistently high food spending leaves little room to clear the savings threshold."

3. Emergency Cushion (emergency_corpus):
 MANDATORY STEP: compute ratio = emergency_corpus ÷ ideal_emergency_corpus. Then look up the tier:
 2.0x or above → "far exceeds the benchmark"
 1.5x to just below 2.0x → "well above the benchmark"
 1.0x to just below 1.5x → "comfortably above the benchmark"
 0.7x to just below 1.0x → "close to the benchmark"
 Below 0.7x → "below the benchmark"

 **WHEN ABOVE BENCHMARK (ratio >= 1.0):**
 - 1.0x–1.5x: pure appreciation — the cushion is solid. No review needed.
 - 1.5x–2.0x: appreciate first, then gently frame the opportunity: "Your emergency cushion is well above the benchmark — there may be an opportunity to put some of it to work elsewhere."
 - 2.0x+: appreciate, then frame the opportunity more clearly: "Your emergency fund far exceeds the benchmark — it could be worth reviewing how much to keep liquid versus exploring options that may earn more."
 Keep the tone warm — this is NOT a criticism. Frame it as an **opportunity to make the most of what they've built**, not a problem.

 **WHEN BELOW BENCHMARK (ratio < 1.0):**
 - Frame as an opportunity: "Your emergency cushion is building — getting it closer to the benchmark can give you more peace of mind."
 - Never say "insufficient," "inadequate," "falling short," or "not enough." Instead use: "still growing," "building toward the benchmark," "room to strengthen."
